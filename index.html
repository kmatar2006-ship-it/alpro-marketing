01 · Understand the target

Four tensions in Alpro's current target definition

Before proposing a refined target, the brand's stated definition needs to be tested against the data. Four tensions surface.

Tension 1, Penetration data contradicts the youth focus

Alpro's brand world skews 18–35, yet 80% of PBB occasions are adult-driven and the +50 cohort holds the largest value share (60.7% of PBB). Alpro's beverage household penetration sits at 8.3% vs the category's 42%, the brand has cracked neither the mainstream nor its stated youth target.

Tension 2, 'Joyfully Healthy' leaves the functional buyer behind

Health is the #1 driver in both PBB (37%) and PBAY (45%). Easy-to-digest is a 32% / 42% driver. Alpro's playful imperfection risks reading as cool but not credible to the buyer with real digestive or cholesterol needs.

Tension 3, 'Inclusive' is structurally incomplete

Top barriers in PBB are Taste (34%), I Prefer Dairy (27%), I Don't Need It (20%). PBAY is 27.9% incremental to dairy, most buyers add rather than switch. Only 1 in 4 PBAY buyers cross-buy formats. Inclusivity should mean converting the dairy lover, not just attracting the converted.

Tension 4, Price is the barrier the category survey under-reports

GFI's top-three barrier list omits price, but its own headline says "price remains a barrier in some categories." In our 25 interviews the picture is sharper: 14 of 19 voices flagged price as a deal-breaker or top-three factor. The Alpro premium versus Hacendado / Yosoy is the single most consistently-cited reason buyers walk past the shelf.

03 · The recommended positioning

One clean positioning statement

After the in-class feedback, we rebuilt the positioning to follow the Session 7 structure. Target, category frame, point of difference, reason to believe. No fuzz, no jargon.

For Spanish adults aged 18 to 49 who want to eat better without making it a personality trait, Alpro is the plant-based brand that delivers real-life moments of joy and health, from your daily breakfast to your weekend treat, because it's the only brand combining 40 years of category leadership in plant-based with the taste and texture you actually want.
Component What it says
Target Spanish adults 18 to 49, focusing on Catalonia at launch. The flexitarian and dairy-curious, not the committed vegan (already converted).
Category frame The plant-based brand for everyday moments and small indulgences, not a specialist health product.
Point of difference Real-life joy and health across every occasion, from breakfast to treat. Both new innovations live inside this frame: the 1kg protein tub for daily ritual, the frozen yoghurt for the treat moment.
Reason to believe Forty years of category leadership, Danone B Corp credentials, and the taste and texture quality that our interviews consistently flagged as the only thing separating Alpro from cheaper private-label options.
So what? Strategic implication
  • Alpro's 'Joyfully Healthy' positioning is culturally differentiated but may under-serve the 35 to 55 consumer whose driver is digestive health, not joy.
  • The white space our interviews reveal is 'Accessible Joy'. A brand that delivers a genuinely premium taste experience without requiring a premium commitment. No major competitor owns this position.
  • Whatever positioning is chosen, it must work at the shelf in 3 seconds, against a Hacendado private-label option that costs 60% less.

This positioning is deliberately less "edgy" than Alpro's current "Joyfully Healthy" wording, but the professor's feedback confirmed our earlier instinct. The brand's biggest weakness is not that it isn't differentiated enough. It's that the differentiation doesn't translate clearly at the shelf or in a thirty-second conversation. Simpler positioning wins the moment of purchase.

5C synthesis: five strategic priorities

Where the five lenses intersect, priorities emerge.

  1. Company × Customers
    Close the penetration gap
    Alpro has dominant brand awareness and market share, yet only 8.3% household penetration. The brand story and cultural resonance need to convert the 'I don't need it' consumer.
  2. Customers × Competitors
    Win on taste parity
    Taste is the #1 driver and the #1 barrier. Both customer and competitor analysis confirm Alpro wins on taste, and loses when it doesn't match dairy or PLB expectations.
  3. Company × Collaborators
    Activate Away From Home
    Alpro's Barista range and the café/restaurant channel are structurally underexploited in Barcelona. The product exists; the collaborator relationships need depth.
  4. Context × Customers
    Serve the older health segment
    Social and demographic context (ageing population, health-first 35–65) points to a large underserved customer group. The brand currently speaks past them.
  5. Competitors × Collaborators
    Defend shelf vs PLB through retailer partnerships
    Cannot win this on price, must win it on shelf placement, promotional visibility, and retailer brand-building investment in Mercadona, Carrefour, Bon Preu.
So what?, Strategic implication
  • Alpro's 8.3% household penetration vs. 42% category penetration is not an awareness problem. It is a trial and conversion problem. People know Alpro; they just keep choosing cheaper.
  • The internal contradiction: 70% of investment goes to product innovation, but the most cited reason consumers don't buy Alpro is price, not product. Are innovation €s solving the right problem?

Method

Semi-Structured In-Depth Interviews, recommended qualitative method per ESADE course material. Audio/video recorded with consent. Verbal AND non-verbal responses captured. Laddering technique used to move from product attributes → functional benefits → personal values (Means-End Chain Theory).

Sample: 5–8 interviews per sub-sample (Peers 18–25 · Family 35–60 · Strangers 25–55), 15–24 total.

Consumer Decision Process, applied to Alpro

The five-stage CDP model maps the journey we want to explore in interviews.

1

1. Problem Recognition

The consumer recognises a need that triggers plant-based consideration.

  • Health trigger: doctor advice, digestive discomfort, desire to reduce dairy
  • Social trigger: friend recommendation, influencer content, café discovery
  • Environmental trigger: reading about dairy's carbon footprint
  • Curiosity trigger: seeing Alpro in a recipe, trying a Barista coffee in a café
  • Practical trigger: lactose intolerance, plant-based household member
2

2. Information Search

The consumer seeks information to evaluate their options.

  • Internal search: prior experience with Alpro or plant-based products
  • Social sources: friends, family, Instagram/TikTok recommendations
  • Retail environment: in-store shelf browsing, product labels, price comparison
  • Digital search: 'leche de avena', 'mejor bebida vegetal', YouTube recipes
  • Café/AFH: barista recommendation; Alpro branding on coffee menus
3

3. Evaluation of Alternatives

The consumer compares options before deciding.

  • Key attributes evaluated: taste/texture, price, health claims, environmental impact, brand familiarity
  • Comparison set: Alpro vs Oatly vs PLB vs dairy with lactose-free option
  • Heuristic shortcuts: 'I always buy Alpro because I know it'
  • Key barriers: 'Alpro is more expensive'; 'I can't tell the difference from PLB'; 'I prefer real dairy'
4

4. Purchase Decision

Affected by in-store cues, availability, and situational factors.

  • Primary channel: supermarket (Mercadona, Carrefour, Bon Preu, Lidl)
  • Secondary channel: online grocery, convenience stores, AFH cafés
  • In-store triggers: promotions, shelf placement, Alpro-branded fridges, recipe suggestions
  • Decision roles: buyer ≠ user (parents buying for children, partners switching the household)
  • Plant-based becomes habitual once established, loyalty is sticky once earned
5

5. Post-Purchase Behaviour

Shapes loyalty, word-of-mouth, and repeat purchase.

  • Satisfaction drivers: taste meets expectations; digestive ease; feeling good about the choice
  • Dissatisfaction drivers: too expensive for daily use; taste mismatch; family rejection
  • Loyalty signals: re-purchase, weekly shopping list, recommendations
  • Advocacy: Instagram, WhatsApp groups, gym/run-club word-of-mouth
  • Dissonance: PLB is much cheaper and 'tastes the same', the premium must be justified post-purchase

Interview question bank, 32 questions, 8 parts

A flexible guide, not a rigid script. Aim for ~12–15 questions per interviewee. Always start with warm-up; end with projective questions when rapport is established.

Part A, Warm-Up & Habits3 questions

Purpose: Build rapport, baseline habits, avoid leading toward plant-based.

  1. Q1, Can you walk me through what a typical weekday morning looks like for you, especially around food and drinks?
  2. Q2, When you're doing your weekly food shopping, how do you decide what to buy? Do you go with a list, or is it more spontaneous?
  3. Q3, How important is food and nutrition to you day-to-day, is it something you think about a lot, or more of a background thing?
Part B, Brand Awareness & Perception5 questions

Purpose: Awareness, recall, emotional associations, Problem Recognition & Information Search.

  1. Q4, When I say 'plant-based drinks' or 'oat milk', what brands immediately come to mind?
  2. Q5, Have you heard of Alpro? What do you know about it, what kind of brand do you think it is?
  3. Q6, If Alpro were a person, how would you describe them? What kind of lifestyle do you imagine they live?
  4. Q7, What words or images come to mind when you think about plant-based food and drinks in general?
  5. Q8, How do you think plant-based products like Alpro compare to traditional dairy, taste, healthiness, value?
Part C, Purchase Motivations & Drivers5 questions

Purpose: Drives consideration and trial, Means-End Chain (attributes → benefits → values).

  1. Q9, Have you ever bought a plant-based drink or yoghurt? What made you try it for the first time?
  2. Q10, What would need to be true for you to swap your usual dairy milk or yoghurt for a plant-based version?
  3. Q11, What matters most: taste, price, health benefits, brand story, or environmental impact? Rank them.
  4. Q12, Has your doctor, a friend, or something you read ever influenced how you think about dairy vs plant-based?
  5. Q13, Think about a moment when you felt really good about what you ate or drank. What made you feel that way?
Part D, Barriers & Objections4 questions

Purpose: Surface real reasons people don't buy.

  1. Q14, Is there anything about plant-based products that puts you off or makes you hesitant to buy them?
  2. Q15, Some people say plant-based milk 'doesn't taste like the real thing.' Does that resonate?
  3. Q16, If Alpro is priced higher than a supermarket own-brand version of the same product, does that change your decision?
  4. Q17, Do you feel like plant-based products are 'for you', or do they feel aimed at vegans / health fanatics?
Part E, Decision & Purchase Process4 questions

Purpose: Map the actual decision journey.

  1. Q18, The last time you bought a plant-based product, can you walk me through exactly what happened?
  2. Q19, Where do you usually discover new food and drink products?
  3. Q20, When you're standing in front of the shelf, what do you look at first?
  4. Q21, Is there anyone in your life who has influenced what plant-based products you buy or try?
Part F, Post-Purchase & Loyalty3 questions

Purpose: Satisfaction, loyalty drivers, advocacy.

  1. Q22, If you've bought Alpro before, did it meet your expectations?
  2. Q23, Do you regularly buy the same plant-based brand, or do you switch around?
  3. Q24, Have you ever recommended a plant-based product to someone else?
Part G, Projective & Values3 questions

Purpose: Beyond rational answers, emotional motivations and personal values (Laddering technique).

  1. Q25, Imagine Alpro as a person at a party in Barcelona. What are they wearing? What are they listening to?
  2. Q26, If you were designing the perfect plant-based drink for yourself, what would it look, taste, and feel like?
  3. Q27, When you make food choices, do they say something about who you are or who you want to be?
Part H, Closing Questions2 questions

Purpose: Catch what was missed; surface advice.

  1. Q28, Is there anything about plant-based products, or specifically about Alpro, that we haven't talked about?
  2. Q29, If you were advising Alpro on one thing they should do differently to win more customers like you, what?
So what?, Strategic implication
  • Semi-structured in-depth interviews were the right methodology here: the laddering technique surfaces values that a survey's closed questions would never reach.
  • The 3-subsample design (peers / family / strangers) was deliberately constructed to test whether findings hold across age and lifestyle groups, and they largely do, with one exception: price sensitivity is higher among the younger cohort.
  • Primary qualitative research is exploratory, not conclusive. Every number from our interviews should be treated as a hypothesis to be validated through a quantitative phase.

11 · Product innovation

Two yoghurt innovations

Alpro already holds 74.1% of plant-based yoghurt value share. But plant-based yoghurt itself only reaches 20.9% of Spanish households, while dairy yoghurt reaches 98.9%. Alpro's challenge is not winning more share inside the category. It's growing the category itself. The two innovations we propose work hand in hand to do that.

A note on the brief

The original PowerPoint brief asks for one yoghurt innovation and one beverage innovation. The teacher clarified that our version is two yoghurt innovations, no beverage. We see this as a stronger play. Plant-based yoghurt is growing faster than beverages (+7% vs. +5.7%), there's no aggressive private-label price war here yet, and the category sits closer to Alpro's brand world. Two yoghurt innovations let us defend the existing buyer base and bring in new buyers at the same time.

The two problems Alpro needs to solve

Plant-based yoghurt has two issues. The first is frequency: even people who buy it treat it as occasional, not daily. The second is trial: 17.9% of Alpro's plant-based yoghurt sales come from under-35s, but under-35s are 30.1% of the population. The brand's stated primary target is the group least represented in actual purchases. Both of our innovations work on both problems at the same time. The protein tub builds frequency by becoming a daily breakfast and post-workout staple, and it also attracts health-curious lapsed buyers. The frozen yoghurt opens a brand-new occasion that pulls in younger consumers, and it also drives repeat buying through summer and dessert moments.

Alpro Skyr +Protein 1kg: revenue calculation

Step 1. Size the addressable segment. The Spanish plant-based yoghurt category is €127M total. The Skyr segment within it is about 9% of value, so roughly €11.4M today. This is the segment we're attacking.

Step 2. Cannibalisation from existing Skyr buyers. Alpro's existing 400g Skyr+Protein buyers will trade up to the 1kg. We assume 25 to 40% of them switch. At Alpro's current Skyr value of around €8.4M (74.1% share of the €11.4M segment), that's €2.1M to €3.4M of cannibalised revenue. But because the 1kg buyer purchases two and a half times the volume per trip, the net incremental from this switch is positive: approximately €4M to €6M from trading up.

Step 3. Incremental household trial. The bigger opportunity is convincing households who buy Greek dairy yoghurt (Pastoret, Hacendado, Danone Oikos) to add the Alpro 1kg to their basket. Spanish households buying Greek dairy total roughly 8 million (rough estimate based on dairy yoghurt penetration of 98.9% multiplied by an estimated Greek-yoghurt share of about 25%). Step 4. Out-of-home and food service. Gym chains, hotels, food service buying the 1kg as a kitchen ingredient. We estimate €2M to €4M, scaling to €5M+ by year three as partnerships mature.

Step 5. Add it up. €4 to 6M + €3.1 to 5.2M + €2 to 4M = €11M (conservative) to €18M (optimistic) in year one.

02
BIG IDEA · GUIDING CREATIVE CONCEPT

"Plant-based, sin drama"

Plant-based, without the drama. The idea that says eating plant-based does not have to be a political statement, a wellness identity, or an apology. It can just be a yoghurt you like.

Why this idea

Most plant-based marketing falls into one of two traps. Either it preaches (saving the planet, animal welfare, your inner discipline), or it apologises (it's almost as good as dairy, we promise). Neither matches how Catalan under-35s actually relate to food. They want it to taste good, they want it to fit their day, and they don't want to be lectured at. "Plant-based, sin drama" gives Alpro a tone that's distinctively Spanish, distinctively Catalan, and distinctively un-preachy. It works across both new products. It works in long-form video, short social, out-of-home posters, and in-store. And it builds directly on Alpro's existing "Joyfully Healthy" and "Purposefully Imperfect" brand pillars without diluting them.

ALPRO SKYR +PROTEIN 1KG

The 1 kg pot becomes the "sin drama protein" answer to overcomplicated fitness nutrition. What we considered and rejected

Four big options we looked at and chose to leave on the table, with the reason in each case.

REJECTED
A celebrity-led TV campaign

The obvious play would be a big TV spot with a Spanish A-lister. We rejected it. TV reach is wasted on an 18 to 34 audience that has largely cut the cord, and the cost per impression is brutal. A creator-led approach with multiple voices is more credible and more efficient.

REJECTED
A pure performance marketing approach

We could have spent the whole €4.5M on paid social and search performance ads optimising for trial. But the underlying brand problem is recall and cultural relevance, not conversion. Performance alone would buy short-term sales without fixing the deeper issue Maya described.

REJECTED
A sustainability-led message

The environmental angle is true and important, but it's not what moves this audience to buy. Our interviews consistently showed that health, taste, and price drove decisions. Sustainability was a "nice to have" mentioned by almost no one as a primary purchase reason. "Sin drama" sidesteps the lecture entirely.

REJECTED
A pan-Spanish identical campaign

Running the same campaign across Madrid, Catalonia, Andalusia, and the Basque Country would have been cheaper. But Catalan culture is genuinely distinct, and Alpro's brand world (Bad Gyal, run clubs, Primavera, Brunch Electronik) is specifically Catalan. We localise it. The same campaign can be adapted for other regions later.

13 · Channel strategy

Where Alpro wins or loses

Most plant-based purchase decisions happen at the shelf, not on a phone. Our interview with David confirmed it: he discovers products in the supermarket, looks at packaging first, and his wife (the lactose-intolerant one) is the actual buyer. Communication builds awareness. The channel converts awareness into a purchase. The two have to be designed together. This section explains exactly how each channel gets activated, which innovation leads in each, and where we put our biggest bet.

Channel design decisions, summarised

Alpro is already an FMCG brand with intensive supermarket distribution. The decision is not whether to be in retail (we are), but how to activate retail differently for each innovation. We also need a deliberate away-from-home strategy because that's where the under-35 consumer actually lives. Our channel design is multichannel and indirect, with intensive coverage in modern trade (Carrefour, El Corte Inglés, Veritas, Bon Preu) and selective coverage in away-from-home (chosen partners, not blanket distribution). The strategy uses pull mechanics (consumer-led demand from the comms campaign) supported by push mechanics (retailer trade investment).

01
THE PRIORITY DECISION

Supermarket is the volume play. Away-from-home is the credibility play.

Most revenue comes from supermarkets. Most brand love comes from away-from-home. We need both, but we activate them very differently.

LEAD CHANNEL · 75% OF REVENUE

Supermarkets (modern trade)

This is where the volume comes from. It's where David, Mariana, Maya, and Marti all said they buy or would buy yoghurt. Our job here is not to chase awareness (the comms campaign does that). Our job here is to make the products findable, attractive at the shelf, and obviously incremental to whatever the shopper came in for. We activate with end-of-aisle displays, branded fridges, in-store sampling, and tactical promotions only at launch (not ongoing, to protect the premium price point).

CULTURAL CHANNEL · 15-20% OF REVENUE, 100% OF BRAND LOVE

Away-from-home (cafés, gyms, cinemas, events)

This is where Alpro becomes a brand people choose, not just a product they buy. The brand world (Honest Greens, Syra Coffee, Brunch Electronik, Primavera Sound, Sunday run clubs) shows exactly where the under-35 Catalan consumer already spends time. Our job here is presence and experience. The frozen yoghurt is the hero here because it travels better into indulgence moments than the protein tub does.

Why we're deferring Mercadona

Launching the new innovations there means competing for shelf space against a private-label version Mercadona will price lower, support harder, and stock more visibly. We launch elsewhere first, build trial and brand demand, then negotiate from a stronger position six to nine months in.

Channel design decisions, mapped to the framework

The same plan, written in the language of the Session 12 channel framework. Each decision below is one of the seven classic channel-design choices, and the column on the right is our specific answer.

Decision (Session 12) Our choice Why
Channel design Multichannel and indirect. We sell through retail partners (supermarkets, cafés, gyms) rather than direct to consumer. Multichannel because we activate physical, digital, and out-of-home together.
Market coverage Intensive in supermarkets. Selective in away-from-home. The yoghurt category is impulse and habit. We need to be everywhere shoppers already go in modern trade. Out-of-home is the opposite: a few carefully chosen partners build more credibility than blanket coverage.
Number of channels Six main channels. Modern trade, premium retail, organic specialist, away-from-home cafés, events and venues, and digital delivery (Glovo).
Ownership All external. No owned retail. Building owned retail (an Alpro flagship store) was considered and rejected (see below).
Push vs pull Pull-led, push-supported. The comms campaign builds consumer demand (pull). Trade investment with the priority retailers (push) makes sure the product is visible when that demand reaches the shelf.
Control of information Medium. We control the messaging in our owned activations (pop-ups, branded fridges, in-store signage) but rely on partner cooperation in cafés, gyms, and supermarket retailer apps.
Cost and profitability Approximately €1.8M in trade investment in year one. Includes listing fees, in-store displays, sampling, trade promotions, and away-from-home partnership setup costs. Recovered through the €19 to 32M revenue opportunity.

What we considered and rejected

Four channel options we looked at and chose to leave on the table, with the reason in each case.

REJECTED
An Alpro flagship store in Barcelona

Tempting, especially given the brand world energy. We rejected it. The setup cost is high (€500K+ for one location), the unit economics are bad for a single yoghurt brand, and Alpro's job is to be everywhere people already shop, not to create a destination. Pop-ups (like the Barceloneta one) deliver the same brand experience at a fraction of the cost.

REJECTED
Direct-to-consumer subscription (alpro.es delivery)

Direct online sales were considered. The problem: the basket size for yoghurt is too small to justify direct delivery economics. We let Glovo and Carrefour Online be the digital storefronts. Alpro doesn't need to own logistics.

REJECTED
Convenience stores (24-hour shops, gas stations)

Real opportunity for the small-format frozen yoghurt cups. But the priority in year one is establishing the two new innovations in the channels where the target consumer already shops. Convenience is a year-two expansion, not a launch channel.

REJECTED
Pharmacy distribution

Some plant-based brands push into pharmacies (high health-credibility signal). We rejected it. Alpro's "Purposefully Imperfect" and "Joyfully Healthy" brand pillars explicitly reject the medicalised wellness aesthetic. Pharmacy distribution would contradict the comms positioning.

If we had to pick one channel to win, it's this one

The brief asks which channel we would prioritise. After all the analysis, the answer is clear.

Priority channel: Carrefour Catalonia
Why: the right scale (intensive coverage), the right shopper (family and 30-49 health-driven), and the right openness to category innovation.
How: end-of-aisle displays for the Skyr 1kg, in-app feature placement, and weekend sampling for the first six weeks. This is our fastest path to trial volume.
Priority cultural anchor: Barceloneta + Honest Greens
Why: the pop-up is the brand moment. Honest Greens is the everyday cultural credibility.
How: together they tell the under-35 Catalan consumer that Alpro is part of their world, not just a yoghurt brand they vaguely recognise.

If the question is "where does the next year of growth come from?", the answer is: Carrefour delivers the volume, Barceloneta delivers the love, and the rest of the channel mix delivers the rest.
